## Slide 4 — SETI Research Background
The Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence, or SETI, has a history spanning more than six decades. It began in 1959 when Cocconi and Morrison published their foundational paper in Nature, proposing that radio telescopes could detect artificial signals from extraterrestrial civilizations. The following year, Frank Drake launched Project Ozma at the Green Bank Observatory, marking the birth of modern SETI.

However, SETI has always faced fundamental difficulties. The search space is extremely large, and the signal form is highly uncertain. Observational coverage has been chronically insufficient, meaning that null results — the fact that we haven't found anything — are difficult to use as strong constraints. The advent of the Square Kilometre Array, or SKA, provides a new entry point for re-evaluating SETI's scientific significance.

---

## Slide 5 — Research Significance & Core Question
The central thesis of my work is: how can SETI transform from a controversial exploratory topic into a testable observational science?

To address this, I treat sky coverage, frequency coverage, and sensitivity as three critical dimensions. I establish a unified observational coordinate system to compare representative programs across different historical stages, and I aim to demonstrate why SKA may serve as the facility inflection point for new-era SETI research.

## Slide 8 — Three-Dimensional Analysis Framework
I propose a three-dimensional analysis framework. The first dimension is sky coverage, measured as cumulative observed sky area in square degrees. For targeted monitoring programs, I approximate this by target count multiplied by primary beam area. The emphasis is on order-of-magnitude comparison.

The second dimension is frequency coverage — the actual searched frequency range in GHz. Importantly, I distinguish between a device's nominal capability and the actual search range implemented by each program. The search bandwidth is the core metric.

The third dimension is sensitivity — the minimum detectable signal capability. I strictly use radio parameters like effective area over system temperature, SEFD, and the radiometer equation. Collecting area in square meters serves as an intuitive proxy in visualizations.

## Slide 9 — Section: Historical Evolution

---

## Slide 10 — Four Stages of SETI Development
I identify four stages in SETI's development.

Stage 1, the 1960s to 1970s, is the Project Ozma era. Cocconi and Morrison's foundational paper in 1959 and Drake's Green Bank observations in 1960 established the narrow-band listening pathway, though academic momentum was slow.

Stage 2, the 1980s to 1990s, is the NASA program era. The High Resolution Microwave Survey represented government-scale commitment. After the Cold War ended, funding was restructured and the Phoenix Project continued the search.

Stage 3, the 2000s to 2010s, is the Breakthrough Listen era. Launched in 2015 with private funding, it brought multi-band coordination, FAST participation from China, and an open data and algorithm renaissance.

Stage 4, the 2020s onward, marks the SKA facility inflection point, where the square kilometre array's sensitivity leap and parameter space constraint capability fundamentally change what null results mean.

---

## Slide 11 — Why Radio Has Long Dominated
A natural question is: why has the radio pathway dominated SETI for so long? There are both physical and technical reasons.

Physically, the radio band passes through Earth's atmosphere with minimal attenuation. Large-aperture radio telescopes provide high sensitivity. Narrow-band filtering is naturally suited for detecting artificially modulated signals. And the interstellar medium attenuates radio frequencies relatively controllably.

Technically, radio astronomy is mature — the Drake Equation provides a quantitative framework. Equipment has been continuously upgraded from the 1960s to the 2020s. Costs are relatively controllable, and facilities can be shared with conventional astronomical observations.

## Slide 12 — Section: SKA's Capability Leap

---

## Slide 13 — Frequency Dimension
Historical programs typically searched only limited frequency bands. This table shows the evolution. Project Ozma used a single frequency. NASA HRMS covered 0.1 to 10 GHz. Breakthrough Listen covers about 2.35 GHz width.

SKA changes the game. SKA1-LOW covers 50 to 350 MHz, and SKA1-MID covers 0.35 to 15.3 GHz. This represents an order-of-magnitude leap in spectral coverage — from individual instrument capabilities to facility-level wideband coverage.

---

## Slide 14 — Sky Area Dimension
Traditional programs were limited. Project Ozma monitored only 2 nearby stars. The Phoenix Project targeted about 800 stars. Breakthrough Listen observed 1,327 stars, covering approximately 0.5 square degrees.

SKA's low-frequency array beam coverage enables true sky surveys. It can repeatedly observe the same regions and accumulate signals. The projected coverage reaches hundreds to thousands of square degrees — a qualitative transformation from targeted monitoring to cumulative coverage.

---

## Slide 15 — Sensitivity Dimension
SKA's value goes beyond simply having a larger aperture. Strict sensitivity comparison uses effective area over system temperature and SEFD. SKA1's total collecting area is approximately one million square meters — one to two orders of magnitude higher than any single-dish telescope.

Comparing across eras: the Green Bank 300-foot telescope had limited collecting area and could only detect nearby strong signals. Parkes and Green Bank under Breakthrough Listen are limited by single-station equipment. SKA1-MID, with its 1,500 dishes each 15 meters in diameter, achieves an SEFD one to two orders of magnitude lower.

---

## Slide 16 — Three-Dimensional Synchronous Expansion
This is my core finding. Most historical SETI programs advanced in only one or two dimensions. For example, Breakthrough Listen achieved good sensitivity but limited sky coverage. NASA HRMS had decent frequency coverage but was a targeted survey.

This table summarizes the comparison. SKA is expected to achieve simultaneous expansion across all three dimensions for the first time — from 0.001 to potentially thousands of square degrees in sky coverage, from less than 0.01 to over 10 GHz in frequency coverage, and from 10 squared to 10 to the fifth meters squared in collecting area.

The breakthrough significance is that this makes long-standing unconstrained null results convertible into definitive parameter space upper bounds.

## Slide 17 — Section: Conclusions

## Slide 18 — Main Conclusions
I have four main conclusions.

First, my literature statistics reveal that SETI's rise and fall are primarily driven by equipment capabilities, funding structures, and related disciplinary developments — not by whether credible signals are discovered.

Second, the three-dimensional framework of sky coverage times frequency coverage times sensitivity effectively reveals the structural differences between historical programs and SKA.

Third, SKA's core significance for SETI lies not in guaranteeing discovery, but in reshaping the evidence base and outcome evaluation methodology.

Fourth, this represents a paradigm transformation — the first simultaneous expansion of three key capabilities, converting null results from weak constraints to definitive parameter space upper bounds.

---

## Slide 19 — Future Perspectives
Looking forward, I identify four directions.

First, commensal observation should be prioritized — SETI backends can operate in parallel with regular astronomical observations without dedicated telescope time.

Second, multi-band coordination — while radio remains the primary axis, optical, infrared, and UV observations provide cross-validation.

Third, algorithm and methods — machine learning and signal processing are core tools for RFI rejection and candidate verification.

Fourth, formalizing null results — treating "no detection" as a formal scientific output with standardized parameter space constraint reporting.

## Anticipated Questions:

1. **"What makes SKA different from just building a bigger telescope?"**
   → It's not just size — SKA achieves simultaneous expansion across all three dimensions (sky, frequency, sensitivity) for the first time, which fundamentally changes what null results mean scientifically.

2. **"Why focus on radio? What about optical SETI?"**
   → Radio remains dominant due to physical and technical advantages (atmospheric transparency, mature technology, narrow-band detection). However, I advocate for multi-band coordination as complementary.

3. **"Is SETI really science if we keep getting null results?"**
   → That's exactly the point of my thesis — the three-dimensional framework shows that SKA can convert null results into meaningful parameter space upper bounds, making SETI a proper observational science.

4. **"How does Breakthrough Listen compare to SKA?"**
   → BL excels in sensitivity but is limited in sky coverage (0.5 deg²). SKA can cover 10²–10³ deg² while maintaining comparable or better sensitivity — a structural, not incremental, improvement.

5. **"What's the realistic timeline for SKA SETI observations?"**
   → SKA is entering construction and early science phase. Commensal SETI observations could begin within the first few years of operation without requiring dedicated time allocation.
